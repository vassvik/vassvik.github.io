## Introduction

The *Problem of Apollonius* is a famous geometrical problem dating back to around 200 BC. The problem can be states as
> Given three circles find a fourth circle that is simultaneously tangent to the other circles. 

Different solutions have been discovered throughout the last couple centuries, ranging from straightedge and compass solutions to brute force arithmetic solutions. The problem can also be formulated as a system of three coupled quadratic equations

$
\begin{align}
  (x_s - x_1)^2 + (y_s - x_1)^2 = (r_s - s_1 r_1)^2 \\label{eq:first}\\\\
  (x_s - x_2)^2 + (y_s - x_2)^2 = (r_s - s_2 r_2)^2 \\label{eq:second}\\\\
  (x_s - x_3)^2 + (y_s - x_3)^2 = (r_s - s_3 r_3)^2 \\label{eq:third} 
\end{align}
$

where $(x_i, y_i)$ and $r_i$ are centers and radii of the three initial circles, and $(x_s, y_s)$ and $r_s$ is the center and radius of the solution circle. The *sign* $s_i = \pm 1$, determines whether the solution circle is *internally* or *externally* tangent to each of the three initial circles.

In general there are 8 unique solutions, one for each combination of sign $s_i$. For degenerate cases where the radius of one of more circles become zero or infinitely large several solutions collapse into one solution. These degenerate cases are often categorized into several subgroups, such as the "circle-circle-point" solution (CCP), the "line-line-point" (LLP) solution, etc.
